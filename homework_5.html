Measures of Location and Online Computation of Mean & Variance

In this assignment, we explore the various ways of computing averages (also called measures of location), understand when they are most useful, and learn how to compute the mean and variance incrementally — that is, in real time as new data points arrive.

1. Common Types of Averages

Several measures have been proposed to represent the “central tendency” of a dataset, each suitable for different kinds of data and distributions:

  • Arithmetic Mean: The sum of values divided by the count. Works best for uniformly distributed numerical data. 
    
values = [2, 4, 6, 8]
mean = sum(values) / len(values)  # 5.0
  • Median: The middle value after sorting. Ideal when the dataset contains outliers that could skew the mean. 
    
values = [1, 2, 100]
median = sorted(values)[len(values)//2]  # 2
  • Mode: The most frequent value. Useful for categorical or discrete data. 
    
from statistics import mode
values = [1, 2, 2, 3]
mode(values)  # 2
  • Geometric Mean: The nth root of the product of n numbers. Appropriate for growth rates or percentages. 
    
import math
values = [2, 8]
geo_mean = math.sqrt(values[0] * values[1])  # 4.0
  • Harmonic Mean: Used when averaging rates or ratios (like speed or density). 
    
import statistics
values = [60, 40]
harmonic_mean = statistics.harmonic_mean(values)  # ≈ 48.0

2. Understanding Mean and Variance

The mean and the variance are the two most fundamental descriptive statistics. They help us understand both the central value and how widely the data points are spread around it.

Mean — the Center of Data

The mean (or average) is calculated by summing all observations and dividing by their number: 


Mean = (x₁ + x₂ + ⋯ + xₙ) / n

For example, for the values 2, 4, and 6, the mean is (2 + 4 + 6) / 3 = 4. This represents the center or expected value of the dataset.

Variance — the Spread of Data

The variance measures how much data points deviate from the mean. While the mean tells us where the center is, the variance tells us how “wide” the data is spread. 


Variance = ( (x₁ - Mean)² + (x₂ - Mean)² + ⋯ + (xₙ - Mean)² ) / (n - 1)

A small variance means most data points are close to the mean (low variability), while a large variance indicates more dispersion (high variability).

Mean vs Variance

The mean shows the center of the data; the variance shows how far values deviate from it. Two datasets can have the same mean but drastically different variances — indicating different levels of consistency or reliability.

3. Online Computation — Mean and Variance in Real Time

In streaming or real-time data systems, recomputing the mean and variance from scratch every time a new value is received would be inefficient. Instead, we use an online algorithm that updates these statistics incrementally.

The interactive tool below lets you input values one by one and immediately see how the mean and variance evolve as new data arrives.

How It Works

The calculator keeps track of three variables: count (number of values), mean (average so far), and M2 (the sum of squared deviations). When a new value x arrives: 


// Add new value x
count += 1;
let delta = x - mean;
mean += delta / count;
M2 += delta * (x - mean);

// Variance formula (for n > 1)
variance = M2 / (count - 1);

This method is known as Welford’s algorithm, which efficiently and accurately updates variance without storing all past data.
