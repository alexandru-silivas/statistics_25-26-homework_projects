Public Key Cryptography and the RSA Algorithm

This assignment explores the concept of public-key encryption through the RSA algorithm. It covers the mathematical foundations of key generation, encryption, and decryption, demonstrating how two keys (public and private) ensure secure data transmission. The project also includes an interactive RSA tool implemented in JavaScript that allows you to generate keys, encrypt and decrypt messages, and visualize letter distributions—mirroring the statistical approach seen in the Caesar Cipher assignment.

1. Understanding RSA and Its Role in Cybersecurity

The RSA algorithm (named after Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman) is one of the cornerstones of modern asymmetric encryption. Unlike traditional ciphers that use the same key for both encryption and decryption, RSA employs a public key and a private key. This innovation allows secure communication even over untrusted networks, since only the private key holder can decrypt the data.

RSA underpins much of today’s secure digital communication: it is used in HTTPS connections, TLS/SSL certificates, digital signatures, and email encryption. When you connect to a secure website, RSA ensures that the data exchanged between your browser and the server cannot be intercepted or read by unauthorized parties.

The algorithm’s strength lies in the mathematical difficulty of prime factorization: while multiplying two large primes to form their product (n = p × q) is easy, reversing the process—determining the primes given only n—is practically impossible with current technology. This one-way property makes RSA both elegant and secure.

In summary, RSA works in three conceptual stages:

  • Key Generation: Two large primes are selected to create the public and private keys.
  • Encryption: The sender uses the public key to convert readable text into ciphertext.
  • Decryption: The receiver uses the private key to reconstruct the original message.

2. How RSA Works — Step by Step

The RSA algorithm builds upon modular arithmetic and the properties of prime numbers. Below is the detailed process:

  1. Choose two distinct prime numbers: Select two prime numbers p and q. They should be large and random to ensure security.
  2. Compute the modulus: Calculate n = p × q. The value n is used in both the public and private keys and defines the size of the key.
  3. Compute Euler’s totient function: Determine φ(n) = (p − 1)(q − 1). This number represents how many integers are coprime to n.
  4. Select the public exponent: Choose an integer e such that 1 < e < φ(n) and gcd(e, φ(n)) = 1. A common choice for e is 65537, as it provides efficiency and security.
  5. Compute the private exponent: Find d such that d × e ≡ 1 (mod φ(n)). This means d is the modular inverse of e modulo φ(n).
  6. Form the key pair: The public key is (n, e) and can be safely shared. The private key is (n, d) and must remain secret.
  7. Encryption: Convert the plaintext message into numerical form (using ASCII codes or blocks of text). Then compute:
    c = me mod n, where c is the ciphertext.
  8. Decryption: Using the private key, recover the message by computing:
    m = cd mod n, obtaining the original plaintext.

These mathematical operations ensure that even if an attacker intercepts the ciphertext and knows the public key, they cannot easily compute the private key or the plaintext, because factoring n into p and q is computationally infeasible for sufficiently large values.

3. Example with Small Primes

Suppose we choose p = 61 and q = 53:

  • n = 61 × 53 = 3233
  • φ(n) = 3120
  • Choose e = 17 → gcd(17, 3120) = 1
  • d = 2753 (because 2753 × 17 ≡ 1 mod 3120)

If we encrypt the message "A" (ASCII = 65), we get c = 6517 mod 3233 = 2790. Decrypting: 27902753 mod 3233 = 65 → "A".

5. Results & Discussion

The RSA encryption algorithm transforms readable text into ciphertext that appears statistically random. Unlike the Caesar cipher or other classical encryption methods, the frequency distribution of the ciphertext bears no resemblance to that of the plaintext. This property prevents attackers from applying frequency analysis—a technique that relies on common patterns in natural language.

As seen above, the letter frequencies of the encrypted text are uniformly distributed, and the χ² (chi-squared) value for the ciphertext is significantly higher than that of typical English text. This confirms that RSA encryption effectively conceals the structure of the original message, making decryption without the private key computationally infeasible.
