Homework 10

Simulating a Counting Process with Rate λ

In this assignment, we simulate a process where events occur independently, uniformly in time, at an average rate $\lambda$ per unit time. This type of model is fundamental in probability theory and lies at the heart of Poisson processes, queueing theory, and stochastic modeling.

To approximate such a continuous-time random process, we divide a time interval (e.g., $T = 1$) into $n$ very small subintervals (e.g., $n = 5000$). During each subinterval, an event occurs with probability $\lambda / n$. As $n \to \infty$, this discrete construction converges to the true Poisson counting process.

1. Discretized Simulation Setup

We approximate the continuous-time model using:

  • Time interval: $T = 1$
  • Number of subintervals: $n = 5000$
  • Event probability per subinterval: $p = \lambda / n$
  • Indicator variable per subinterval: $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$

The number of events in $[0,1]$ is then:

$N(1) = \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$

As $n$ grows larger, $N(1)$ converges in distribution to a Poisson($\lambda$) random variable.

2. What Process Are We Approximating?

The limiting process is the Poisson Counting Process, $\{N(t), t \ge 0\}$, which satisfies:

  • Independent increments
  • Stationary increments (distribution depends only on time difference)
  • $N(t) - N(s) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda(t-s))$
  • $N(0)=0$ with probability 1

In other words, it models the number of “arrivals” or “successes” happening over time — often used in network traffic, radioactive decay, arrivals in queues, failures of components, and more.

3. The Meaning of the Rate Parameter λ

The constant $\lambda$ determines the expected number of events per unit time:

$\mathbb{E}[N(1)] = \lambda$

Higher $\lambda$ means more frequent events. It also controls the variance:

$\mathrm{Var}(N(1)) = \lambda$

The Poisson process is one of the rare stochastic processes where mean = variance — a key fingerprint used in statistical detection and model validation.

5. Interpretation

This discrete-time construction illustrates how a Poisson process arises not as a “mysterious” continuous object, but as the natural limit of many tiny Bernoulli trials. This mirrors the same convergence ideas explored in Homework 4 (Law of Large Numbers): small random pieces combining to reproduce deterministic behavior (the average rate λ), while still retaining inherent randomness (the Poisson distribution).

The Poisson process stands as a fundamental stochastic model: combining simplicity, interpretability, and strong mathematical structure — and appearing everywhere from physics to cybersecurity.
