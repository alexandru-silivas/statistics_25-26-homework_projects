Assignment 1 — The Meaning of “Converge”

In probability theory, when we say that “the frequency converges to the probability,” we mean that as the number of trials of a random experiment increases, the observed frequency of an outcome approaches its theoretical probability.

For instance, if we repeatedly toss a fair coin, we expect that about half of the outcomes will be heads. In the short term, this ratio might fluctuate (e.g., 3 heads in 5 tosses, or 47 heads in 100 tosses), but as the number of tosses grows, the relative frequency of heads will get closer and closer to 0.5 — it converges to the true probability.

Assignment 2 — The Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) formalizes this concept mathematically. It states that as the number of independent trials increases, the sample mean of observed outcomes approaches the expected value. In other words, randomness “averages out” over time, and results become more stable and predictable.

There are two main forms:

  • Weak Law: The probability that the sample mean deviates significantly from the expected value becomes smaller as the sample size increases.
  • Strong Law: The sample mean actually converges to the expected value with probability 1 as the sample size approaches infinity.

This concept explains why empirical frequencies can be used to estimate true probabilities, and why statistical averages become more reliable with larger datasets — a cornerstone of data science, signal processing, and cybersecurity analytics.
