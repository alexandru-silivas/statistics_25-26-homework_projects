Homework 8

Bernoulli Processes, Random Walks, and the Structure of Binomial Mathematics

This homework ties together several ideas encountered previously: Bernoulli trials, the Law of Large Numbers (HW4), Random Walks (HW7), and combinatorial structures such as binomial coefficients, Pascal’s Triangle, binomial expansion, Fibonacci recursion, and more.

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent trials where each trial results in a success (1) with probability \(p\) or a failure (0) with probability \(1-p\). If instead of 1 and 0 we assign \(+1\) to success and \(-1\) to failure, the Bernoulli process becomes a symmetric or asymmetric random walk.

This is exactly the same mechanism encountered in Homework 7 when modeling weekly server security: a secure week counted as \(+1\), a breach counted as \(-1\), and the total score behaved as a random walk driven by Bernoulli trials.

In Homework 4, we used Bernoulli trials to illustrate the Law of Large Numbers. Here we extend that connection: the number of successes after \(n\) Bernoulli trials follows a binomial distribution, whose coefficients appear in Pascal’s Triangle (Tartaglia). These coefficients also govern the binomial expansion and are closely related to Fibonacci numbers through diagonal summations.

Pascal’s (Tartaglia’s) Triangle and Binomial Coefficients

Pascal’s Triangle encodes the binomial coefficients \(\binom{n}{k}\) that appear in:

\[ (a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^{k}. \]

It is also the counting structure underlying the binomial distribution \(B(n,p)\), which determines the probability that a Bernoulli process has \(k\) successes in \(n\) trials:

\[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}. \]

Bernoulli Processes and Random Walks

Assigning \(+1\) to a success and \(-1\) to a failure transforms a Bernoulli process into a random walk. If \(p = 0.5\), the walk is symmetric and stays near 0 with high probability. If \(p \neq 0.5\), the walk drifts upward or downward.
