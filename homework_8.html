Bernoulli Processes, Random Walks & Their Combinatorial Structure

This assignment compares the Bernoulli process, the random walk, and the combinatorial structures that govern them: binomial coefficients, Pascal‚Äôs triangle (Tartaglia), the binomial expansion, and links to sequences such as the Fibonacci numbers.

1. Bernoulli Process vs Random Walk

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent trials where each trial results in success (1) with probability $p$ and failure (0) with probability $1-p$. The random variable is:

$$ X_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{with probability } p \\ 0 & \text{with probability } 1-p \end{cases} $$

In contrast, a random walk uses values $+1$ and $-1$ instead:

$$ Y_i = \begin{cases} +1 & \text{with probability } p \\ -1 & \text{with probability } 1-p \end{cases} $$

The key insight is that random walks are just Bernoulli processes under a linear transformation:

$$Y_i = 2X_i - 1$$

This means:

  • A Bernoulli process tracks proportions of success.
  • A random walk tracks the cumulative deviation from balance.

2. Pascal‚Äôs Triangle, Binomial Coefficients & Fibonacci

Both processes are governed by the same combinatorial structure: the binomial coefficients. They appear in:

  • Pascal's triangle (Tartaglia)
  • binomial distributions
  • binomial expansions via $(a+b)^n$
  • combinatorial counting problems
  • Fibonacci recursion along shallow diagonals

3. Connecting Homework 7 and Homework 4 ‚Äî A Deep Explanation

Homework 4 and Homework 7 describe what is fundamentally the same probability experiment, expressed in different mathematical languages. Below we explain why their graphs behave similarly and how the underlying theory links Bernoulli trials, random walks, binomial coefficients, and Pascal‚Äôs triangle.

3.1 Bernoulli Processes (HW4 context)

Homework 4 showed how relative frequencies converge to a true probability. Given Bernoulli trials $X_i$, the frequency is:

$$\hat{p}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$

and the Law of Large Numbers guarantees:

$$\hat{p}_n \to p$$

3.2 Random Walks (HW7 context)

Homework 7 used values $+1$ (secure week) or $-1$ (breach). The cumulative security score was:

$$S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$$

This produces a trajectory interpreted as a ‚Äúwalk‚Äù.

3.3 Why These Are the Same Experiment

Because $Y_i = 2X_i - 1$, the random walk is literally a rescaled Bernoulli process:

$$S_n = 2K_n - n,\qquad K_n = \sum X_i$$

Thus:

  • HW4 tracks the proportion of successes.
  • HW7 tracks the cumulative difference between successes and failures.

Same data ‚Üí different viewpoint.

3.4 The Binomial Distribution Controls Both

The random walk position $S_n$ is directly tied to the binomial count $K_n$:

$$P(S_n = s) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k},\qquad k = \frac{n+s}{2}$$

This explains the shape of the histograms in both assignments.

3.5 Pascal‚Äôs Triangle as the ‚ÄúShape of All Possibilities‚Äù

Each row of Pascal‚Äôs triangle counts all ways a random walk can reach each position.

  • Row $n$ corresponds to all $2^n$ paths of $n$ steps.
  • Entry $\binom{n}{k}$ counts paths landing at position $S_n = 2k - n$.

The triangle is the combinatorial skeleton underlying both HW4 and HW7.

3.6 Convergence Laws in Both Contexts

HW4 showed:

$$\hat{p}_n \to p$$

HW7‚Äôs equivalent statement for the random walk is:

$$\frac{S_n}{n} \to 2p - 1$$

Same convergence phenomenon ‚Äî different scale.
