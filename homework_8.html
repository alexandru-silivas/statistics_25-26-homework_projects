Homework 8

Bernoulli Processes, Random Walks, Binomial Mathematics, and Their Connections

This assignment unifies all major ideas from Homework 4 (Law of Large Numbers via Bernoulli trials) and Homework 7 (Random Walks in cybersecurity scoring). The goal is to understand that **Bernoulli processes, binomial distributions, Pascal’s Triangle, random walks, and Fibonacci sequences are not separate ideas — they are different faces of the same underlying combinatorial structure**.

1. Bernoulli Processes

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent trials where each trial has:

\[ X_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{with probability } p, \\ 0 & \text{with probability } 1-p. \end{cases} \]

In Homework 4 we used Bernoulli trials to show that as the number of trials increases, the empirical frequency of successes converges to the true probability \(p\). That is the **Law of Large Numbers (LLN)**.

\[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \;\xrightarrow[]{n\to\infty}\; p. \]

2. Random Walks

A random walk simply assigns \(+1\) to success and \(-1\) to failure:

\[ S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i,\qquad Y_i = \begin{cases} +1 & \text{with probability } p, \\ -1 & \text{with probability } 1-p. \end{cases} \]

Homework 7 used this idea to model a server’s security “score” over multiple weeks. When \(p=0.5\), the walk is symmetric and hovers around 0. When \(p<0.5\), the walk drifts downward. When \(p>0.5\), the walk drifts upward.

3. Binomial Distribution and Pascal/Tartaglia Triangle

The number of successes in \(n\) Bernoulli trials is a binomial random variable:

\[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}. \]

The coefficients \(\binom{n}{k}\) appear visually in Pascal’s Triangle (Tartaglia’s Triangle), where each entry is the sum of the two above it. This structure also encodes the binomial expansion:

\[ (a+b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k. \]
