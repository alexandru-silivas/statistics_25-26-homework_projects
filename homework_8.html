Homework 8

Bernoulli Processes, Random Walks, Binomial Coefficients, Pascal’s Triangle & Fibonacci

This assignment compares Bernoulli processes with random walks, highlights their visual similarities and differences, and explores their deep mathematical connections with binomial coefficients, Pascal’s triangle (Tartaglia’s triangle), the binomial distribution, binomial expansion, and even the Fibonacci sequence.

The previous homework (Homework 7) involved simulating security-breach random walks, while Homework 4 focused on the Law of Large Numbers through Bernoulli and frequency convergence. This homework ties these topics together and shows how the same mathematical objects — binomial coefficients — sit underneath all of them.

1. Bernoulli Process vs Random Walk

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent experiments, each producing a 1 (success) or 0 (failure). When we transform successes into +1 and failures into –1, we obtain a symmetric random walk. This is exactly what we did in Homework 7: each secure week = +1, each breach = –1.

The Law of Large Numbers (as studied in Homework 4) governs Bernoulli processes: as the number of trials increases, the empirical frequency of successes converges toward the true probability \(p\). In Homework 7, we instead looked at cumulative sums (random walks), but the underlying Bernoulli mechanism is the same.

2. Pascal’s Triangle (Tartaglia) & Binomial Coefficients

Each row of Pascal's Triangle corresponds to the coefficients of the binomial expansion:

\((a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k\)

In probability, the binomial distribution is:

\(P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}\)

Both Homework 4 (Bernoulli frequencies) and Homework 7 (random walk scores) trace back to these same coefficients. Pascal’s Triangle effectively counts the number of possible paths that lead to a given outcome.

3. Fibonacci & Pascal

One of the most beautiful mathematical facts is that the Fibonacci sequence appears in Pascal's triangle: summing along the shallow diagonals yields:

\(1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, ...\)

This further illustrates how random-walk paths, Bernoulli processes, combinations, and classical number sequences are all deeply linked through combinatorics.
