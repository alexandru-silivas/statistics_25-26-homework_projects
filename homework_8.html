Homework 8

Bernoulli Processes, Random Walks, and Their Deep Mathematical Connections

In Homework 4, you simulated a Bernoulli process and observed how the Law of Large Numbers causes empirical frequencies to converge to theoretical probabilities. In Homework 7, you simulated a random walk generated by weekly server attacks—where each week is either a success (+1) or a failure (–1).

This homework connects these two worlds. A Bernoulli process and a random walk are fundamentally two views of the same mathematical object. Their behavior is governed by:

  • Binomial coefficients
  • Pascal's Triangle (Tartaglia)
  • Binomial expansion
  • Fibonacci-like combinatorics
  • Combinatorial coefficients

This page gives a clear explanation of these concepts, how they relate, and why they matter.

1. Bernoulli Process vs. Random Walk

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent trials where each trial has:
Success with probability \( p \), failure with probability \( 1 - p \).

A random walk takes this exact Bernoulli sequence and transforms it:

\[ X_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} Y_i,\qquad Y_i = \begin{cases} +1 & \text{with prob } p \\ -1 & \text{with prob } 1-p \end{cases} \]

The same underlying process; two interpretations.

2. Binomial Coefficients, Pascal’s Triangle, and Random Walks

The probability of getting exactly \( k \) successes in \( n \) Bernoulli trials is: \[ P(K=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \]

These coefficients \( \binom{n}{k} \) appear in:

  • Binomial distribution
  • Random walk score probabilities
  • Bernoulli process frequencies
  • Pascal’s triangle (Tartaglia)

3. Fibonacci Connections

Pascal’s Triangle contains the Fibonacci sequence embedded in its diagonals. This happens because Fibonacci numbers count restricted lattice paths — a combinatorial object deeply related to walks on a grid.

4. Connecting Homework 7 and Homework 4

Homework 4 used a Bernoulli process to show the Law of Large Numbers. Homework 7 used a random walk to simulate weekly system breaches.

These two are mathematically equivalent:

  • HW4 studies frequencies of successes.
  • HW7 studies the cumulative sum of successes and failures.
  • Both rely on binomial probabilities.
  • Both match theoretical curves predicted by Pascal’s triangle.
  • Both converge to their expected values as the number of trials grows.

So, HW4 and HW7 are simply two perspectives on the same phenomenon.
