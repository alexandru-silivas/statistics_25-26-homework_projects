Homework 8

Bernoulli Processes, Random Walks, Pascal's Triangle, and Combinatorics

This homework compares the Bernoulli process studied in Homework 4 and the random-walk simulations of Homework 7, showing how both are manifestations of the same underlying mathematical structure: the binomial distribution and its combinatorial foundations.

1. Bernoulli Processes vs Random Walks

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent trials, each producing either a success (1) with probability p or a failure (0) with probability 1 − p. In Homework 4, we observed how the relative frequency of successes converges to p as the number of trials grows — a direct demonstration of the Law of Large Numbers.

A random walk, as introduced in Homework 7, is obtained by mapping each Bernoulli trial to a step: +1 for success and −1 for failure. The cumulative sum of steps produces a trajectory over time.

What appears to be two different processes is actually the same object viewed from two perspectives: Bernoulli → counts; Random walk → cumulative signed displacement.

Bernoulli: K successes after n trials
Random walk: Final position S = #successes − #failures = 2K − n

This connection means that the distribution of final random-walk scores is completely determined by the binomial distribution, which in turn is determined by binomial coefficients.

2. Pascal’s Triangle (Tartaglia) & Binomial Coefficients

Pascal’s triangle is a triangular array of binomial coefficients. Each entry represents the number of ways to choose k successes in n trials:

C(n, k) = n! / (k!(n − k)!)

Each row of the triangle represents the coefficients of the binomial expansion:

(p + (1 − p))ⁿ = Σ C(n, k) pᵏ (1 − p)ⁿ⁻ᵏ

These coefficients count the number of possible sequences with exactly k successes — the building blocks of both Bernoulli-process frequencies and random-walk final-score distributions.

3. Deep Analogy Between Homework 4 & Homework 7

The Law of Large Numbers from HW4 and the random-walk simulations from HW7 are two faces of the same coin.

Bernoulli → LLN (Homework 4)

In HW4, repeated Bernoulli trials showed:

frequency → probability

As the number of trials grows, randomness “smooths out” and outcomes stabilize.

Bernoulli → Random Walk (Homework 7)

In HW7, the same trials were interpreted as +1 or −1 steps. Instead of frequency convergence, we studied:

distribution of final scores

The “bell-shaped” histograms in HW7 were simply the binomial distribution expressed in walk coordinates.

How Pascal Ties Them Together

  • Pascal’s triangle counts paths in the random walk.
  • These counts determine the binomial distribution.
  • The LLN describes convergence to the same distribution.
  • The binomial expansion algebraically encodes the whole process.

Even Fibonacci appears because its diagonals count restricted classes of walk paths.

Conclusion: HW4 (LLN) describes the frequency behavior of Bernoulli trials. HW7 (random walk) describes the combinatorial behavior. Pascal’s triangle is the bridge between the two.
